# 01

This prenatal class covered a wide range of details, from preparing for labor to medical interventions during birth. Below is a summary of the key points based on the class notes:

## I. When to Go

- 
    
    **Timing contractions (5-1-1):** Go to the hospital when contractions are coming every **5 minutes**, each lasting **1 minute**, and this pattern has continued for **1 hour**.
    
- 
    
    **No speech rule:** If contractions are so intense that you **cannot talk or breathe through them**, go in right away regardless of the timing.
    
- **What to do if your water breaks:**
    - 
        
        **Before 37 weeks:** This is a sign of preterm labor, so you must go to the hospital immediately.
        
    - 
        
        **At term (37+ weeks):** If you are not having contractions yet, you can watch and wait for a bit. But if the amniotic fluid **smells bad, has an abnormal color (possibly containing meconium)**, or the birthing parent is **GBS positive**, you must go in right away for antibiotics.
        
    - 
        
        **How to check:** If you are not sure whether your water has broken, put a tissue in your underwear, stand up, lift your belly, and cough. If fluid gushes out, your water has likely broken.
        
- 
    
    **Warning symptoms even outside labor:** A persistent headache, sudden swelling of the face or limbs, spots in your vision, itchy feet, fever, vomiting, or burning with urination (a urinary tract infection) all warrant medical evaluation.
    

---

## II. Prep Checklist

- **Mental and environment prep:**
    - Finish all your "loose ends" (such as taxes, work handoff, or even a haircut) so those small tasks do not become mental obstacles during labor.
    - 
        
        **Nesting instinct:** Set up at least one room that feels comfortable and safe to you.
        
- **Things to prepare:**
    - 
        
        **Car seat:** Install it ahead of time and practice using it (YouTube tutorials are recommended). When leaving the hospital, the baby should not wear a bulky snowsuit in the seat, because the straps can slip.
        
    - 
        
        **Hospital bag:** Pack hand cream, comfortable pajamas, and a water bottle with a straw.
        
    - 
        
        **Food:** Prepare shelf-stable snacks and frozen broth (beef, chicken, or vegetable, with ginger and honey) so it can be heated at the hospital.
        
- 
    
    **Postpartum planning:** Look into Galactagogues, arrange your baby's doctor, and confirm whether insurance covers breast pump rental.
    

---

## III. Triage

- **Labor assessment (Triage):**
    - 
        
        **Do not stay seated the whole time:** Walking, using a birth ball, or getting back massage can help labor progress more than sitting still.
        
    - 
        
        **Fetal heart monitoring:** Baseline monitoring usually takes 30 minutes. If the baby is asleep and heart-rate variability is low, you can drink **apple juice or chew ice** to wake the baby up.
        
    - 
        
        **Admission threshold:** You are usually admitted to a labor room once you are **3-4 cm** dilated and contractions are strong and regular.
        
- **Mental coping techniques:**
    - 
        
        **Eyes Up:** Looking upward when you are thinking can help your brain shift into a more creative, relaxed state.
        
    - 
        
        **Sticky-note method:** Write down fun things you want to do with your baby after birth on sticky notes, put them on the wall during labor, and use them as focus points for a sensory deep-breathing meditation.

## IV. Interventions

### Epidural

- 
    
    **Best timing:** When you feel yourself shifting **from "coping" to "suffering"** (often around 3 cm). Getting it too early (for example, at 0 cm) may increase the risk of C-section by **50%** because it limits the birthing parent's ability to move.
    
- **Side effects and risks:**
    - 
        
        **Position changes:** You need to turn about every half hour to prevent temporary postpartum leg numbness caused by nerve compression.
        
    - 
        
        **Fluid effects:** A temporary catheter is needed to empty the bladder so the baby can descend.
        
    - 
        
        **Spinal headache:** In very rare cases, this can happen and may require treatment with a **Blood Patch** procedure.
        
- 
    
    **Special limitations:** People with large tattoos on the back (especially with dark ink directly over the puncture site) or prior spinal fusion surgery may not be able to get an Epidural.
    

### Pitocin

- 
    
    **Use:** If contractions are not strong enough or labor stalls, the doctor may use Pitocin to increase their frequency and strength.
    
- 
    
    **Monitoring:** Continuous fetal monitoring is required with Pitocin because it can put stress on the baby. If the baby does not tolerate it well, the nurse will temporarily stop the medication.
    

---

## V. Hospital Team

- 
    
    **Who may be in the room:** You may encounter Residents or medical students. You have the right to ask who they are, request that a student leave the room, or ask for a more experienced nurse to help.
    
- 
    
    **Important note:** Some hospitals, such as Sunnybrook, do **not** provide newborn circumcision. If this matters for cultural reasons, contact an outside clinic in advance.
