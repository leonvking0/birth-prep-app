## I. Question Checklist

The instructor invented the surgical term **"Whoo-siotomy"** to remind parents that when a doctor suggests any medical procedure, such as a C-section, induction, or episiotomy, they should not accept it blindly just because they have heard the term before. Instead, they should ask questions based on their own situation:

- **What is this procedure?** How exactly is it done?
- **Why are you recommending it for me?** What is the doctor's reasoning and what are the specific indications?
- **What are the risks and benefits?**
- **Are there any alternatives?** (For example, instead of proceeding right away, could I go home, eat some fiber-rich food, or keep observing?)
- **If I do not do it, what is the worst-case scenario?**
- **Who will perform it?** (A senior doctor or a first-year medical student?)
- **How long is the recovery?** Will it affect future pregnancies or births?

## II. Monitoring and Mobility

- **Continuous monitoring vs. intermittent monitoring:** Continuous fetal heart monitoring can lead to more "false positives," which may raise the C-section rate. For low-risk birthing parents, listening with a Doppler every 15 minutes may be better.
- **When continuous monitoring is required:** When there is Meconium in the amniotic fluid.
- **Wireless monitor (Telemetry):** Many hospitals, including Sunnybrook, offer wireless monitoring so the birthing parent can get out of bed, walk around, or even shower. The instructor noted that nurses may not offer it proactively because it is less convenient, so **birthing parents should ask for wireless Telemetry themselves** in order to stay mobile.

## III. IV and Emergencies

- **When an IV is needed:** For antibiotics if you are GBS positive, for induction, for medications, or for an Epidural.
- **The debate around a preventive IV:** Doctors sometimes place one early "just in case" of an emergency C-section. The instructor pointed out that a true Crash C-section is extremely rare, and even without an IV, doctors can respond quickly with mask anesthesia in a real emergency.
- **Mobility tip:** If you do need an IV, you can ask for it to be placed higher up on the arm rather than in the hand or wrist, which makes it easier to move.

## IV. Induction

Induction means starting labor artificially before spontaneous labor begins.

- **Common reasons:** Fetal growth restriction (SGA), gestational diabetes (a very large baby, such as the 98th percentile), advanced maternal age (over 40), or maternal mental health stress.
- **Medical vs. emotional factors:** Anything from 37-42 weeks is considered term. Average pregnancy length also differs across ethnic groups, with East Asian pregnancies averaging around 39 weeks.
- **Methods of induction:**
    1. **Cervical ripening (the longest stage):**
        - **Misoprostol:** Used in small, titrated doses and acts directly on the cervix, so it does not necessarily cause intense contractions.
        - **Foley catheter (balloon):** Physically dilates the cervix and stimulates your body's own hormone release.
        - **Cervidil:** A vaginal insert with a slightly higher risk of triggering contractions.
    2. **Artificial rupture of membranes:** If the cervix is already 2 cm dilated, the water can be broken directly together with Pitocin.
- **What to expect mentally:** Induction is a slow process. While waiting in Triage at the hospital, parents who are already in active labor take priority, so induction patients may face a long wait. **Suggestion: bring snacks and movies.**

---

## V. Augmentation

- **Pitocin:** The instructor shared a case in which a doctor may push for more Pitocin because their own shift is ending soon.
- **The right to refuse:** If both the birthing parent and baby are doing well and labor is still progressing, the birthing parent has the right to refuse Augmentation.
- **Body signals:** If the birthing parent feels a strong urge to poop or starts making involuntary throat sounds, such as a stressed "Ah," it means the baby is descending and the nurse should be asked to check, even if a few hours earlier the cervix was only 6 cm.

---

## VI. Special Cases

- **Breech position:** A doctor may try an ECV. The instructor suggested that the success rate is higher when the entire body is fully relaxed, such as through hypnobirthing training.
- **Forceps vs. C-section:** Forceps are usually attempted only when the chance of success is very high, around 90%.
- **Long-term impact of a C-section:** In Canada, after a C-section, it is recommended to wait 18-24 months before the next pregnancy. This matters a lot for families planning closely spaced births, such as those using frozen embryo transfer.

---

## VII. Partner Support

- **Reducing anxiety:** A lot of anxiety before labor comes from "waiting" and the "unknown." Once the birth process starts, that anxiety often shifts into action.
- **Counter Pressure:** If the birthing parent has back pain, especially with back labor, the partner can press on the bones near the back pocket area while the birthing parent leans forward to help relieve pain and encourage a better position.
- **Breathing cue:** The instructor mentioned a technique for moments when the birthing parent is not ready yet but cannot help wanting to push: have them say the word "House," because it is hard to push while making that sound.

---

## Summary Recommendations:

1. **Trust your body:** Listen to the body's signals that it wants to push downward.
2. **Keep moving:** Ask for wireless monitoring whenever possible and try different positions, such as squatting, sitting on a ball, or even sitting on the toilet.
3. **Communicate proactively:** When an intervention is suggested, calmly use the question checklist.
